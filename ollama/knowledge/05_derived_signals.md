# GRID Key Derived Signals

These are GRID's most valuable composite signals — each has a specific economic mechanism and documented lead time.

## China Credit Impulse

**Calculation**: 12-month change in Total Social Financing (TSF) / GDP
**Source**: AKShare (China macro data)
**Lead Time**: 6-12 months ahead of global growth

### Why It Matters
China's credit cycle is the largest swing factor in global demand. When Chinese authorities loosen credit (TSF accelerates), the effects ripple through:
1. Chinese industrial production rises → commodity demand increases
2. Infrastructure spending accelerates → copper, iron ore rally
3. Import demand grows → benefits export-oriented economies (Korea, Germany, Australia)
4. Global PMIs follow with a 6-12 month lag

### How to Read It
- **Impulse turning positive**: Global reflation trade starting — go long commodities, EM, cyclicals
- **Impulse peaking**: Best of the growth impulse is behind — start rotating to quality
- **Impulse turning negative**: Global slowdown ahead — defensive, reduce commodity exposure
- **Impulse deeply negative**: Either bottom is near (contrarian) or structural deleveraging (dangerous)

## K-Shape Ratio

**Calculation**: High-income consumer spending minus low-income consumer spending
**Source**: Opportunity Insights (weekly, 2020+)
**Signal**: Structural inequality / K-shaped recovery indicator

### Why It Matters
Post-COVID, recoveries have been uneven. The K-Shape Ratio reveals whether growth is broad-based or concentrated:
- **Widening**: Only high-income consumers spending — fragile recovery
- **Narrowing**: Broad-based spending — sustainable growth
- **Important**: Traditional macro data (GDP, retail sales) averages over this and hides the divergence

## Korea Export YoY

**Source**: KOSIS (Statistics Korea)
**Lead Time**: First major economy to report monthly trade, Week 1 of following month
**Signal**: Global trade cycle early read

### Why It Matters
South Korea reports export data faster than any other major economy, and Korean exports are heavily weighted toward semiconductors, electronics, and petrochemicals — making them a proxy for global tech demand and trade health.
- **Korea exports accelerating**: Global trade cycle improving
- **Korea exports decelerating**: Global demand weakening
- **Semiconductor exports specifically**: Leading indicator for global tech capex cycle

## VIIRS-Macro Divergence

**Calculation**: NASA nighttime lights intensity vs official industrial production
**Source**: NASA VIIRS (monthly, 2012+)
**Signal**: Data quality flag

### Why It Matters
Nighttime light intensity from satellite imagery correlates with real economic activity and is impossible to manipulate. When official statistics diverge from VIIRS data:
- **Lights brighter than stats suggest**: Official data may be understating activity (or informal economy growing)
- **Lights dimmer than stats suggest**: Official data may be overstating activity (particularly relevant for China, India)
- **Both aligned**: Higher confidence in official statistics

## Patent Velocity

**Calculation**: Application rate by CPC (Cooperative Patent Classification) class
**Source**: USPTO PatentsView (annual, 1976+)
**Lead Time**: 2-3 years ahead of capex cycles

### Why It Matters
Patent application volume by technology class reveals where R&D spending is concentrated. This predicts capital expenditure cycles because:
1. Companies patent innovations before commercializing them
2. Rising patent velocity in a sector → rising capex in that sector 2-3 years later
3. Particularly useful for tech hardware, biotech, clean energy, and manufacturing sectors

## BTP-Bund Spread

**Calculation**: Italy 10Y yield minus Germany 10Y yield
**Source**: ECB SDW
**Signal**: Euro area stress barometer

### Why It Matters
The BTP-Bund spread is the single best indicator of euro area fragmentation risk:
- **<150 bps**: Normal conditions, ECB credibility intact
- **150-250 bps**: Elevated concern, ECB likely paying attention
- **>250 bps**: Crisis territory, ECB intervention likely
- **Rapid widening**: Political risk or banking stress in Italy — contagion risk to broader Euro area

## OECD CLI Slope

**Calculation**: 3-month rate of change of OECD Composite Leading Indicators
**Source**: OECD SDMX (44 countries, 1970+)
**Signal**: Regime transition early warning

### Why It Matters
The CLI is constructed from a broad set of leading indicators across 44 countries. Its slope matters more than its level:
- **Slope turning positive from negative**: Recovery beginning — early cycle opportunity
- **Slope turning negative from positive**: Expansion peaking — late cycle positioning
- **Slope accelerating positive**: Growth broadening — risk-on
- **Slope accelerating negative**: Recession risk — risk-off

## OFR Financial Stress

**Source**: Office of Financial Research
**Components**: Credit sub-index, funding sub-index, leverage sub-index
**Update**: Weekly

### How to Read the Components
- **Credit stress rising**: Default risk increasing, corporate bond market under pressure
- **Funding stress rising**: Interbank/repo market tightening — liquidity crisis risk
- **Leverage stress rising**: Margin calls, deleveraging — can cascade quickly
- **All three rising simultaneously**: Systemic stress — highest alert level
