# Market Analysis Framework

When generating market briefings, follow this structured approach.

## Briefing Structure

### 1. Regime Assessment (Lead With This)
- What regime are we in? (Risk-On, Risk-Off, Transition, Crisis, Recovery)
- How confident is the classification?
- How long has this regime persisted?
- Any signs of impending transition?

### 2. Cross-Asset Signal Check
Walk through each feature family systematically:

**Rates**: Yield curve shape, real rates, Fed trajectory
**Credit**: Spread levels and direction, IG vs HY
**Equity Breadth**: Participation, momentum, internals
**Volatility**: VIX level, term structure, realized vs implied
**FX**: Dollar direction, implications for global liquidity
**Commodities**: Copper/gold ratio, energy, agricultural
**Macro**: PMI, LEI, inflation trajectory

### 3. Contradiction Scan
Actively look for signals that disagree:
- Which families are confirming the regime?
- Which families are diverging?
- What would resolve the contradiction?
- Which signal historically leads?

### 4. International Context
- China credit impulse direction
- OECD CLI slope across major economies
- Korea exports trend
- Euro area stress (BTP-Bund)
- EM capital flows direction

### 5. Risk Assessment
- What is the biggest risk the market is NOT pricing?
- What would cause a regime transition?
- Where are positioning extremes?
- What is the asymmetry of outcomes?

### 6. Actionable Takeaway
- Clear regime classification
- Confidence level (LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH)
- Key levels or events that would change the view
- Time horizon for the assessment

## Tone Guidelines

- **Be precise**: Use specific feature names and values when available
- **Be honest about uncertainty**: If signals are mixed, say so
- **Think in probabilities**: "70% chance we remain risk-on" not "we are definitely risk-on"
- **Mechanisms over patterns**: Always explain WHY, not just WHAT
- **Flag what you don't know**: If data is missing or stale, say so explicitly
- **Historical context**: Reference past regimes with similar configurations
- **No hype**: Avoid superlatives. "Significant move" not "MASSIVE UNPRECEDENTED move"

## Common Analytical Errors to Avoid

1. **Recency bias**: Don't overweight the last data point
2. **Narrative fitting**: Don't construct a story to match your conclusion
3. **False precision**: Don't give exact levels when ranges are more honest
4. **Ignoring base rates**: Most days are normal, most moves are noise
5. **Confusing correlation with causation**: Two things moving together doesn't mean one causes the other
6. **Survivorship bias**: Remember the signals that failed, not just the ones that worked
7. **Anchoring**: Don't compare everything to the last crisis or the last bull market
