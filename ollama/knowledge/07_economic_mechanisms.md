# Economic Transmission Mechanisms

Understanding HOW signals propagate through the economy is critical for interpreting regime changes. This reference maps the key transmission channels.

## Monetary Policy Transmission

```
Fed raises/lowers FFR
  → Short-term rates move immediately
  → Long-term rates move based on expectations (may invert)
  → Dollar strengthens/weakens (interest rate differential)
  → Corporate borrowing costs change (credit channel)
  → Housing activity responds (mortgage rate channel)
  → Consumer spending adjusts (wealth effect channel)
  → Employment follows (Phillips curve, with significant lag)
  → Inflation responds (long and variable lags, 12-24 months)
```

**Key timing**: Policy → financial conditions (immediate) → real economy (6-12 months) → inflation (12-24 months)

## Credit Cycle Transmission

```
Credit conditions tighten
  → Bank lending standards rise (Senior Loan Officer Survey)
  → Corporate bond spreads widen
  → Leveraged borrowers face higher costs
  → Capex plans get deferred/cancelled
  → Hiring slows
  → Consumer confidence drops
  → Spending contracts
  → Earnings decline
  → Default rates rise
  → Credit tightens further (reflexive feedback loop)
```

**Key warning signs**: HY spreads widening while equities are still near highs = credit leading equities lower

## Global Dollar Transmission

```
USD strengthens
  → Dollar-denominated debt becomes more expensive for EM
  → EM central banks forced to defend currencies (raise rates)
  → Commodity prices fall (priced in USD)
  → US multinationals see earnings headwind (translation effect)
  → Global trade contracts (financing costs rise)
  → EM capital outflows accelerate
  → Further USD strengthening (reflexive loop)
```

**Key dynamic**: Strong USD is deflationary for the world and tightens global financial conditions even without Fed action

## China Credit Impulse Transmission

```
China loosens credit (TSF accelerates)
  → Infrastructure spending rises
  → Property construction activity increases
  → Industrial metals demand surges (copper, iron ore, aluminum)
  → Korean/German exports to China rise
  → Global PMIs improve (6-12 month lag)
  → Commodity currencies strengthen (AUD, BRL, ZAR)
  → EM equities outperform
  → Global reflation narrative takes hold
```

**Key timing**: TSF acceleration → commodity prices (3-6 months) → global PMIs (6-12 months) → developed market earnings (9-15 months)

## Yield Curve Transmission

```
Yield curve inverts (short rates > long rates)
  → Bank net interest margins compress
  → Bank lending becomes less profitable
  → Credit availability tightens at the margin
  → Curve inversion persists for months
  → Curve STEEPENS (un-inverts) — THIS is the recession signal
  → Un-inversion usually means Fed is cutting (late)
  → Recession typically starts 0-6 months after un-inversion
```

**Critical insight**: Inversion is the WARNING. Un-inversion (steepening from inverted) is the SIGNAL that recession has arrived or is imminent.

## Volatility Regime Transmission

```
VIX spikes (vol shock)
  → Risk parity funds delever (mechanical selling)
  → Systematic strategies reduce exposure (vol targeting)
  → Market makers widen spreads (liquidity withdrawal)
  → Correlation increases (diversification fails)
  → Margin calls force additional selling
  → Bid-ask spreads widen further
  → Liquidity black hole (reflexive feedback)
  → Eventually stabilizes when forced selling exhausts itself
```

**Key dynamic**: Vol-of-vol matters more than vol level. A VIX at 25 that's been there for weeks is very different from a VIX that just spiked to 25 from 13.

## Trade Cycle Transmission

```
Global trade slows
  → Export-oriented economies slow first (Korea, Germany, Taiwan)
  → Shipping rates decline (Baltic Dry Index)
  → Port congestion decreases (AIS vessel data)
  → Commodity demand drops
  → EM currencies weaken
  → Eventually feeds back to US through multinational earnings
  → And through financial conditions (EM stress → risk-off globally)
```

**Key leading indicators**: Korean exports → Baltic Dry → Global PMIs → US ISM

## Inflation Transmission

```
Inflation accelerates
  → Real wages decline (nominal growth < inflation)
  → Consumer purchasing power eroded
  → Central bank tightens (raises rates)
  → Financial conditions tighten
  → Duration assets sell off (bonds lose value)
  → Equity multiples compress (discount rate rises)
  → Eventually demand destruction occurs
  → Inflation peaks and declines
  → Central bank can ease
```

**Key distinction**: Demand-pull inflation (healthy, often accompanies growth) vs cost-push inflation (unhealthy, stagflationary)
